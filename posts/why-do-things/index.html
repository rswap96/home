[Draft2]

Given that things are worth doing and one is skilled, honest and brave enough to pursue action, there are three fundamental questions that need to be addressed - what, why and how to go about this often noble - but always valiant - enterprise of action.

The What is an important question in that it reflects what the doer deems as the most pertinent and valuable thing to do among all the things that can be done. The hero then refers established wisdom, seeks mentorship and summons the internet to devise a plan of action - the How. This scheming, popularly perceived as one of the most important things, often amounts to very little (if anything), because thinking is worth nothing and doing is worth everything.

In this post, I argue that the cardinal question to dwell upon is Why1 - or the correct motivations for action. We will evaluate all the popular ends and decide:

  • Which ends are more valuable than others (yes, some ends are, in fact, objectively, greater than other ends)
  • Which value systems are superior

I will also touch why I think a well-formulated, individualistic value system is indispensable for leading a meaningful life. All of this, ultimately, rests upon the assumption that you are interested in leading a meaningful life and that such a thing and such a life actually exists.

Why is this post relevant?

Or - Should you read this post?; Will this make you ‘better’?

It’s not, you shouldn’t and no it won’t. This discussion is entirely devoid of any real-world or any other world utility…well, at least to most people. For me it underscores everything I decide to do (and more importantly - not to do) in my life. This would sound gibberish to you if you have never thought along these lines.

I spent the most exciting time of my life studying accounting, costing and taxation. I hated almost every second of it. I was interested in everything else but this (isn’t this always the case? :p) - literature, cinema, science, computers…The objective of my efforts were very clear to me at every stage, at a micro level - the specifics of doing well in a course. I did it in the best possible way. But I had no good reason of doing any of it apart from the reason that something needs to be done.

Barring survival and duty I don’t see any other reason for you to do something that don’t make you forget (and even relish) the morbidness of existence. This only makes sense if you actually think your time and life valuable.

I make case for this general approach:
> You have to decide to do something 
> You check your long-term aims 
> You have a hierarchy of values > 

As against this more prevalent one:
> You have to decide to do something 
> You consult the contemporary list of honorable and desirable aims
> You pick one end based on its community value and your convenience

A Bold Assumption

All of this post (and everything else) assumes that -

  1. Things/actions have value
  2. Some things/actions are more valuable than others

This is a dangerous assumption that is far from obvious.
- It is not certain that doing things other than for survival is inherently valuable - The case for action is weak - Given no justice system or moral conscience - there’s absolutely no reason to do the right thing (or to do anything at all) - We should be aware of this before venturing any further - In light of this it becomes very important for us to recognize our motivations and assess which are better than others - This of course will only make sense to you if you think some things are better/more valuable than some other things and vice versa
